The current VCSF pipeline relies on global Wavelet Scattering Transform (WST) coefficients, which aggregate information across the entire map. While effective at capturing non-Gaussianity, this global pooling likely washes out the localized, high-frequency "baryonic imprints" that are most sensitive to hydro-code differences. I hypothesize that **spatially-resolved local scattering features** (computed on sub-patches or via a convolutional scattering network) will significantly improve OoD detection sensitivity. By replacing global pooling with a spatially-aware representation—specifically, a **Patch-based Conditional Neural Spline Flow (P-CNSF)**—we can detect localized structural anomalies (e.g., specific peak morphologies or filamentary distortions) that are currently masked by the global average. This approach will be implemented by dividing the 2D-reconstructed maps into a grid of overlapping patches, computing WST coefficients for each, and training the CNSF to estimate the joint density of these local features conditioned on the global cosmological parameters ($\Omega_m, S_8$). This local-to-global aggregation will allow the model to flag maps containing even small, localized regions of "hydro-code mismatch" that would otherwise be diluted in a global feature vector, thereby increasing the TPR in the low-FPR regime.
