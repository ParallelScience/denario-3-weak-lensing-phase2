The current VCSF approach is a significant step forward, but the reliance on a GMM for density estimation in a 100-dimensional feature space is a major bottleneck. GMMs struggle with the "curse of dimensionality" and are unlikely to capture the complex, non-linear manifold of the convergence field, leading to the observed mediocre partial AUC (0.0235).

**Critical Weaknesses:**
1. **Density Estimation Mismatch:** A GMM assumes the conditional distribution $p(x|\theta)$ is a mixture of Gaussians. Given the non-Gaussian nature of weak lensing maps (skewed, heavy-tailed), this is a poor inductive bias. The "synthetic OoD" validation (quadratic transformation) is too simplistic; it does not capture the subtle, multi-scale morphological differences inherent in hydro-code mismatches.
2. **Feature Selection Bias:** Selecting the top 100 WST coefficients based on SNR (cosmological vs. nuisance variance) is clever but potentially discards the very features where the hydro-code mismatch is most prominent. If the hydro-code difference is orthogonal to the cosmological parameter variation, your SNR metric might be filtering out the signal.
3. **Inference Pipeline:** The ensemble approach (5 samples from the regressor) is a heuristic for uncertainty that does not account for the aleatoric uncertainty of the map itself.

**Actionable Recommendations:**
1. **Replace GMM with a Normalizing Flow (NF):** Since you have `zuko` installed, replace the GMM with a Conditional Masked Autoregressive Flow (MAF) or a Neural Spline Flow. NFs are far more expressive and better suited for the non-Gaussian, high-dimensional structure of WST coefficients. This will likely improve the partial AUC significantly.
2. **Refine the Validation Strategy:** The quadratic transformation is not a realistic proxy for hydro-code mismatch. Instead, use the "Leave-One-Cosmology-Out" (LOCO) approach to create a *true* OoD proxy: train on 80 cosmologies and test on the remaining 21. This tests the model's ability to generalize across cosmologies while detecting structural anomalies, which is a much harder and more relevant task.
3. **Feature Sensitivity Analysis:** Instead of discarding coefficients with low SNR, use a dimensionality reduction technique like UMAP or a simple Autoencoder bottleneck to compress the 417 WST coefficients. This preserves information that might be critical for OoD detection but was deemed "noisy" by your SNR metric.
4. **Direct Likelihood Ratio:** If you can train two flows—one on the full training set and one on a subset—you can compute the likelihood ratio directly. This is often more robust for OoD detection than NLL, as it naturally cancels out common nuisance-induced variations.
5. **Simplify the Uncertainty:** The "errorbar" calculation is currently a heuristic. If you use a Flow, the model provides a direct estimate of the log-likelihood. Use the variance of the log-likelihood across different noise realizations of the *same* map as your uncertainty estimate, rather than sampling the regressor posterior.

**Forward-Looking Insight:**
The project should pivot from "parameter estimation + density estimation" to "direct conditional density estimation." By training a conditional flow $p(x | \Omega_m, S_8, T_{AGN}, f_0, \Delta z)$ directly on the WST features, you eliminate the regressor's error propagation entirely. This is more robust, simpler to implement, and aligns better with the goal of detecting structural anomalies.
