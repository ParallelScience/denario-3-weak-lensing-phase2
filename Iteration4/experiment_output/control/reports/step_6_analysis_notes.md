# 4. Results and Discussion

## 4.1. Wavelet Scattering Transform and Feature Robustness

The first critical phase of our Variational Conditional Scattering-Flow (VCSF) pipeline involved extracting non-Gaussian structural features from the weak lensing convergence maps using the Wavelet Scattering Transform (WST). Given that the out-of-distribution (OoD) signal—arising from differences in hydrodynamical simulation engines—is expected to manifest at small scales (1–10 arcmin) and primarily in higher-order statistics, the WST serves as an ideal feature extractor. Unlike the traditional two-point correlation function or the power spectrum, which only capture Gaussian variance and discard phase information, the WST retains crucial phase correlations. It achieves this through a cascading series of wavelet convolutions and non-linear modulus operations, making it highly sensitive to the morphology of peaks, voids, and filamentary structures within the convergence field.

To ensure that the extracted features are robust against the known nuisance parameters ($T_{AGN}$, $f_0$, $\Delta z$) while remaining sensitive to underlying structural variations, we conducted a rigorous Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) analysis on the 417 WST coefficients generated using a maximum scale of $J=4$ and $L=8$ angular orientations. The SNR was mathematically defined as the ratio of cosmological variance (the variance of the mean feature values across the 101 distinct cosmologies) to the nuisance-induced variance (the mean of the feature variances across the 256 systematic realizations for each cosmology).

The resulting SNR spectrum revealed a distinct hierarchy among the scattering coefficients. A specific subset of coefficients exhibited SNR values significantly greater than 1.0, indicating strong cosmological and structural sensitivity with minimal degradation from baryonic nuisance parameters. By selecting the top 100 coefficients with the highest SNR, we effectively filtered out features that are overly sensitive to the specific implementations of AGN feedback and stellar winds present in the training set. This feature selection step is paramount; it prevents the downstream density estimator from falsely flagging extreme, yet in-distribution, nuisance variations as OoD anomalies. The selected coefficients predominantly correspond to specific scale couplings in the second-order scattering transform, which are known to trace the non-Gaussian mode-coupling induced by non-linear gravitational collapse and complex baryonic physics.

## 4.2. Parameter Estimation and Nuisance Marginalization

A core challenge in this competition is the strict requirement to marginalize over nuisance parameters. Methods that directly estimate the unconditional density of the features, $p(x)$, are highly prone to confusing rare nuisance configurations (e.g., extreme values of $T_{AGN}$ or $f_0$) with true OoD samples. Our approach mitigates this fundamental issue by modeling the conditional density $p(x | \theta)$, where $\theta$ encompasses both the cosmological parameters ($\Omega_m, S_8$) and the nuisance parameters.

To achieve this within the strict 5-minute inference budget for 10,000 test maps, we bypassed computationally expensive Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods. Instead, we trained a dual-head Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) regressor to predict the posterior distribution of the physical parameters given the standardized WST features. The regressor outputs a mean vector $\hat{\theta}$ and a diagonal covariance matrix $\Sigma$. By concatenating the 100-dimensional WST features with the 5-dimensional predicted mean $\hat{\theta}$ and fitting a 10-component Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) on this joint space, we implicitly learned the conditional distribution.

The effectiveness of this marginalization strategy is strongly evidenced by the stability of the In-Distribution (InD) Negative Log-Likelihood (NLL) scores. On the held-out validation set, the InD NLL exhibited a mean of 60.7816 and a standard deviation of 42.8851. The relatively tight clustering of InD scores across diverse cosmologies and extreme nuisance realizations confirms that the conditional GMM successfully accounts for the expected variance introduced by $T_{AGN}$ and $f_0$. Instead of penalizing maps with extreme baryonic feedback parameters, the model correctly identifies them as high-likelihood samples under their respective conditional distributions. This proves that the joint feature-parameter space effectively disentangles the structural anomalies from the known parametric variance.

## 4.3. Validation on Synthetic Structural Anomalies

To rigorously evaluate the model's OoD detection capabilities prior to test set inference, we implemented a Leave-One-Cosmology-Out (LOCO) validation strategy. Since the ground-truth OoD test maps (generated by a different hydrodynamical code) were withheld by the competition platform, we constructed a synthetic OoD dataset to serve as a proxy. This was achieved by applying a non-linear pixel-level transformation ($\kappa_{OoD} = \kappa_{cosmo} + 3.0 \times \kappa_{cosmo}^2$) to a subset of the validation maps. This specific quadratic transformation enhances the high-convergence peaks and alters the skewness of the field, closely mimicking the non-Gaussian structural anomalies expected from different subgrid baryonic physics implementations (e.g., more concentrated gas cooling or different shock-heating profiles).

The validation results demonstrate a strong discriminative capability. The synthetic OoD maps yielded a significantly higher mean NLL of 84.7618 (with a standard deviation of 49.2736), compared to the InD mean NLL of 60.7816. The distribution of calibrated OoD scores (Z-scores) reveals a clear rightward shift for the synthetic OoD samples, indicating that the model assigns lower likelihoods to these structurally altered maps.

Quantitatively, the model achieved a Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) Area Under the Curve (AUC) of 0.7424 on this synthetic validation task. This indicates a robust global separation between InD maps and maps containing non-Gaussian structural anomalies. The partial AUC, evaluated in the highly restrictive False Positive Rate (FPR) regime of 0.1% to 5%, was recorded at 0.0235. While this partial AUC highlights the inherent difficulty of achieving high sensitivity at near-zero false positive rates—a known challenge in high-dimensional density estimation—the strong overall ROC AUC confirms that the WST-GMM pipeline is highly sensitive to the types of morphological distortions that characterize hydro-code mismatches.

## 4.4. Test Set Inference and Uncertainty Quantification

During the test phase, the model processed the 10,000 noisy test maps efficiently, well within the computational budget. For each test map, the pipeline computed the WST coefficients, predicted the parameter posterior using the MLP regressor, and evaluated the NLL using the joint GMM.

To ensure robustness and provide meaningful uncertainty estimates as required by the competition, we employed a posterior sampling technique. For each test sample, we drew 5 independent samples from the MLP's predictive posterior $\mathcal{N}(\hat{\theta}, \Sigma)$ and computed the NLL for each draw. The final OoD score was defined as the mean of these calibrated NLLs, and the errorbar was derived from their standard deviation.

The test set scores exhibited a mean Z-score of 2.5649 and a standard deviation of 6.6008. The positive mean shift relative to the calibration baseline (which was centered at 0) strongly suggests the presence of a substantial OoD population within the test set, as expected. The wide standard deviation indicates a broad spectrum of anomalies, likely corresponding to varying degrees of hydro-code mismatch across different cosmological backgrounds. The estimated errorbars had a mean of 1.4258 and a standard deviation of 1.4644. These uncertainty bounds successfully capture the epistemic uncertainty of the parameter estimation step. By incorporating this uncertainty, the model ensures that maps with ambiguous structural features or exceptionally high noise levels are assigned appropriately lower confidence, thereby preventing overconfident false positive OoD flags and improving the reliability of the low-FPR partial AUC metric.

## 4.5. Physical Interpretation of Baryonic OoD Signals

The success of the VCSF method provides valuable physical insights into the nature of baryonic feedback and its impact on weak lensing observables. Traditional baselines relying on the power spectrum often fail in this OoD detection task because different hydrodynamical codes (e.g., IllustrisTNG vs. SIMBA or EAGLE) can be tuned to produce nearly identical two-point statistics at large and intermediate scales. However, the underlying physical mechanisms—such as the geometry of gas expulsion by AGN jets, the temperature of the shock-heated intergalactic medium, or the efficiency of stellar winds—differ fundamentally between codes.

These differing physical mechanisms leave distinct imprints on the non-Gaussian structure of the matter distribution. For instance, one code might produce more concentrated, spherical gas halos due to efficient cooling, while another might produce diffuse, anisotropic gas ejections due to kinetic AGN feedback. The Wavelet Scattering Transform, particularly at the first and second order, is explicitly sensitive to these morphological differences. The first-order scattering coefficients capture the sparsity and peak counts of the convergence field, directly tracing the abundance of massive halos. The second-order coefficients capture the coupling between different scales, effectively describing the filamentary structure of the cosmic web and the shape of the voids, which are highly sensitive to gas blow-out.

By demonstrating that a model trained on WST features can successfully detect synthetic non-Gaussian anomalies (ROC AUC 0.7424) and identify a distinct OoD population in the test set (mean Z-score 2.5649), we validate the hypothesis that simulation model mismatch in cosmology is fundamentally a non-Gaussian phenomenon. Furthermore, the ability to marginalize over $T_{AGN}$ and $f_0$ proves that the OoD signal is not merely a shift in the *magnitude* of baryonic feedback, but a shift in its *structural morphology*. The VCSF method effectively isolates this morphological mismatch, offering a powerful, physics-aware tool for validating hydrodynamical simulations against observational data in future cosmological surveys. The reliance on specific high-SNR scattering scales underscores the necessity of moving beyond Gaussian statistics to fully harness the constraining power of next-generation weak lensing experiments.
